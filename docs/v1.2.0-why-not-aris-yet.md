# v1.2.0 Why Not ARIS Yet

Status: interview narrative.

Round: 253.

## The Decision

ARIS is a strong future reference, but it is intentionally deferred from v1.2.
The v1.2 priority is original-reference parity first.

## Why Deferral Was The Better Engineering Call

ARIS-style features are attractive because they point toward deeper research
review loops. They are also higher-risk:

- cross-model review can create opaque authority chains;
- meta-optimization can silently change behavior;
- proof-checking can make correctness feel stronger than the evidence supports;
- paper-claim audit can blur review aid versus final claim generation;
- paper resubmission workflows can push the project toward premature paper
  automation.

Putting those into v1.2 would make the system more impressive on paper but less
stable in practice.

## How Deferral Avoids Premature Complexity

The deferral keeps v1.2 focused on capabilities that can be locally tested:

- fake/default public demos;
- deterministic stress tests;
- explicit runbooks instead of execution;
- contracts and workflow tests;
- no default networking;
- no automatic result promotion;
- no unknown plugin or remote execution.

This makes the project easier to run and easier to explain.

## v1.3 And v1.4 Plan

- v1.3: study ARIS patterns and design safety boundaries.
- v1.4: consider absorbing a small number of mature ARIS-inspired capabilities
  only if they pass design review, safety review, and regression gates.

## Interview Answer

"I deferred ARIS because the base system needed reference parity first. ARIS is
interesting, but cross-model review and proof-checking introduce authority and
claim-safety risks. I wanted v1.2 to be boringly reliable: offline by default,
tested, documented, and clear about what is demo versus verified live work.
That is the kind of tradeoff I would make in a real team too."
